you're scrolling through reddit or your friends won't shut up about f1 and now you're here. maybe you watched a race and had no idea what a grid walk was. maybe you're confused about tire colors. this is the post for you.

let's break down how a race weekend actually works, because it's not just sunday. it's a whole thing.

**thursday: nobody really cares yet**

media day. drivers and team people sit down for interviews. the social media admins film content that will get 40k likes on instagram. it's not required viewing. honestly, skip it unless you're already obsessed.

**friday: practice, which is exactly as boring as it sounds**

two one-hour sessions. fp1 (first free practice) and fp2 (second free practice). teams are just getting the cars running, testing setups, collecting data. occasionally you'll see a rookie or a formula 2 driver in fp1 to see what they can do, but that's about it.

(on sprint weekends, which happen six times a year, this changes. fp2 gets replaced by sprint qualifying. more on that later if you care.)

**saturday: the day that matters**

one more practice session (fp3), then qualifying. this is where the grid for sunday gets decided.

qualifying works like this: it's split into three parts. q1 eliminates the 5 slowest drivers. q2 eliminates another 5 (out of the 15 remaining). q3 is just the 10 fastest drivers throwing down one more lap to arrange themselves 1st to 10th.

"pole position" is 1st. it's the best spot on the grid. no points for getting it (which honestly should change, but the fia won't listen to me), but it matters tremendously the next day.

you might see fernando alonso in an amr25 here, and he'll probably be somewhere he shouldn't be based on pure pace alone, because that's what fernando does.

**sunday: the actual race**

about 40 minutes before anything happens, the commentators do a grid walk. they walk from the back of the grid to the front, talking about each driver, what went wrong this weekend, and what tires they're starting on.

then there's a formation lap (slow lap to heat up the tires and get everyone in the right order), five red lights go out, and lights out and away we go.

**tires: yes, they matter more than you think**

there are three main slick compounds:

softs (red): fastest, wear out quickly. good for short bursts.

mediums (yellow): middle ground. honest work.

hards (white): slow, last forever. boring, but sometimes necessary.

two wet tires exist too: intermediates (greens) and full wets (blues). rain happens, tires change accordingly.

every track has its own optimal strategy. what works at monaco (short, slow, low deg) is nothing like what works at silverstone (long, fast, high deg). strategy is half the race, and mclaren somehow still gets it wrong half the time.

races are 50-70 laps depending on track length. almost always around 2 hours start to finish.

factor in tire deg, pit stops, safety cars, whether the fia decides to be consistent with penalties (spoiler: they won't), and you've got a race.

**after it's over**

top three get trophies. the winner's national anthem plays. they spray champagne. it's the bit everyone remembers.

that's the basic structure. there's way more to it (drs, track limits, team radio, strategy madness), but this is the foundation. once you understand how a weekend flows, the chaos makes more sense.
