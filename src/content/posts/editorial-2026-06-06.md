max verstappen is currently seventh in the championship with 43 points. seventh. we need to sit with that for a moment since the sentence feels like it belongs in an alternate timeline, not the actual 2026 F1 season.

this is the same driver who won three consecutive world championships. who dominated entire seasons so thoroughly that commentators ran out of adjectives. who made moves that seemed physically impossible because he understood the car the way most drivers understand how to hold a steering wheel. and now he's collecting 43 points like everyone else while a rookie in a Mercedes is building a 43-point gap on the second-place driver.

the weird thing is: Max isn't driving badly. Red Bull is just... done. whatever magic Newey had in those cars has evaporated completely (and honestly, it probably evaporated the moment he left). the RB21 looks like it was designed by someone who watched F1 highlights once and then took a very long nap. Max is extracting everything from an underpowered, overweight car that seems fundamentally confused about its own concept, and he's still regularly in the points (mostly). that's skill. it's just not enough to matter when you're driving what is essentially a car that forgot how to be competitive.

but let's talk about the other thing nobody wants to say: this might be Max's ceiling now. not his driving ceiling (that remains impossibly high), but his championship ceiling. Red Bull's organization structure has issues that Max can't fix from the cockpit. you can't out-drive a constructor championship deficit of 162 points. you just can't. he'd need Ferrari and McLaren to simultaneously combust, and even then Mercedes would still be there (currently, they're 219 points ahead. two hundred nineteen).

the truly dark part is watching rookies and second-year drivers lap him on pace. Andrea Kimi Antonelli is leading the championship by 43 points over George Russell, and Antonelli is driving like he's been doing this his whole life instead of his first year. Oscar Piastri is 48 points ahead of Max despite driving for a team that makes strategy decisions like they're drawing them from a hat. these aren't hypothetical comparisons—these are actual point totals on an actual scoreboard.

and here's the thing that keeps me up (it's 3 AM on a Friday in Riyadh, so: relatable): Max deserves better than this. you can have whatever opinion you want about his personality or his on-track aggression or whether he's actually the greatest driver alive (he's not; that's Alonso, forever), but the man is genuinely one of the top three drivers in the world right now. watching him languish in a car that can't deliver is like watching prime Messi play for a regional Italian team. the skill is still there. the car isn't.

Monaco tomorrow will be interesting specifically because it'll show us whether Max can still pull off that impossible moment he's famous for. tight tracks, high stakes, cars that can't hide problems. if anywhere is going to let a driver overcome machine deficit, it's Monaco. but 43 points into a 26-race season is a lot of ground to make up, and Red Bull shows no signs of fixing this. they've had five rounds to figure it out.

maybe they're just broken.
